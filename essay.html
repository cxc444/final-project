Examining the Effects of U-Boat Warfare

The turn of the 20th century brought massive changes to our world as we know it. From mass production of consumer goods, advancements in modern medicine, science, technology, and more. These new technologies included advancements in naval vessels as well as new vessels entirely. One such vessel was the submarine. The first submarine to be conceptualized was from British mathematician and writer on naval subjects, William Bourne. He had proposed a completely enclosed boat that could be submerged and rowed underwater; the concept consisted of a wooden frame covered with waterproof leather which would allow the craft to be submerged by reducing its volume by contracting the sides through the use of hand vises. Bourne never constructed his idea of what a submarine could be and thus is not credited with building the first submarine; that accomplishment was achieved by Dutch inventor Cornelius van Drebel. Between 1620 and 1624, he successfully maneuvered the craft at depths from 12-15 feet beneath the water’s surface during repeated trials in the Thames River. Many others patented their new submarine designs, particularly during the early 18th century. One such unidentified inventor proposed an ingenious method of submerging and returning to the surface. This submarine design had goatskin bags attached to the hull with each skin connected to an aperture in the bottom of the craft. The plan was to submerge the vessel by filling the skins with water and to surface by forcing the water out of the skins with a “twisting rod”. This arrangement was a forerunner of the modern submarine’s ballast tank. Though this design never was submerged to test it out. As submarine technologies advanced so did the use of submarines in other aspects of naval life, such as warfare. The submarine was first used as an offensive weapon in naval warfare during the American Revolution (1775-83). The submarine was called the Turtle, a one-man craft invented by David Bushnell. The submarine was built of wood in the shape of a walnut standing on its end and when submerged it was powered by propellers cranked by the operator. The purpose of the Turtle was that it would make an underwater approach to a British warship, attach a charge of gunpowder to the ship’s hull by a screw device that was operated within the craft and leave before it exploded. Though the Turtle did not succeed as it was unable to force the screw through the copper sheathing on the warships hull. The Turtle propelled the way forward for other submarines to be built and participate in naval warfare. With the new advancements in how submarines were constructed, powered, and equipped it would change the ways that naval warfare and wartime strategies were developed. One development in particular that shifted wartime strategies was Germany’s use of U-Boat submarines in the first and second world war.  It is important to note that U-Boats mainly patrolled on the surface of the water, but had the ability to submerge underwater and cruise at lower speeds. These U-Boats were used to disrupt trade routes between the US and allies via the act of “ship hunting”, as well as engaging in several battles with naval vessels around the world. Our project focuses on how U-Boats affected maritime activity off the New Jersey coast and provoked new technological developments such as radar and sonar.  We analyzed the dataset made available by the New Jersey Maritime Museum, which contains around 4500+ entries of shipwrecks off of the New Jersey Coast. This dataset includes shipwrecks from 1705-2013; 308 years of shipwrecks that include both purposeful sinking of ships for the artificial reef, and other ships that were wrecked due to various or unknown reasons. The dataset contains five data points that detail German U-Boats operating off the coast of New Jersey during WWI and WWII. These U-Boats torpedoed a number of ships listed in the database, as well as many others around the world. In addition to the data set, we have gathered a number of sources, primarily from a naval standpoint, that document how U-Boats were combatted and strategies developed in response. Through our project, we aim to clarify what impacts the U-Boats had on technological advancements of the time, naval strategy, trade/commerce, and overall maritime activity.

The first major event in the timeline of U-Boat history was Black Sunday, on June 2, 1918. This was the first time that the Germans attacked American cargo ships. On this day, the U-151 sunk six ships and damaged two or more, including the liner SS Carolina,  freighters Winneconne and Texel, and the schooner Isabel B. Wiley. Before sinking them, the U-151 fired off warning shots and allowed people to exit the vessels beforehand. The combined crew and passengers were about 448 people, and the casualties were 13 people, due to one of the SS Carolina’s lifeboat’s capsizing. This was the first time that German U-Boats inhabited US waters off the East Coast, and the first foreign naval attack near the US border since the War of 1812.

Within the literature and information provided so far, it has been consistent with the documentation of the German U-Boats engaging in activities in the Atlantic Ocean and off of the New Jersey Coast. There is an abundant amount of information that is presented through books, articles, archives, photographs, military documents and more. In particular the US Navy released documents about German U-Boat activities in the Atlantic involving the United States and Canada. It documents U 151, one of the U-boats in the dataset, providing information on how long it took to travel from Germany to the Atlantic coast and vice versa. It covers a variety of topics, counter tactics, the effects on the maritime economic activities in the Atlantic Coast. This in depth set of information gives us access to understand why German U-Boats wanted to come to the East Coast and why it attacked the ships it did. The information from the US Navy also includes the ships that the U 151 sunk, which are also included in the dataset. Each ship is explored in detail on how it was sunk and how it impacted the activities of the US East Coast. The included photos help give context and visualization to the information that is being presented. It is important to note that U 151 was the only German U-Boat to be documented from World War I and was the first German U-Boat to breach US waters since the War of 1812.

        Additionally, British and Canadian naval history sources go into detail regarding the use of the Enigma encoder, part of the Allied intelligence project Ultra, that was used to break Nazi code in order to anticipate their positions and strategies. It is argued in these sources that no discussion of this topic would be valid without considering the influence of Ultra on allied strategies and their inevitable triumph in WWII. This technique allowed for allied forces to intercept German transmissions and pinpoint German U-Boats in an attempt to destroy the fleets. Additionally, it could be stated that the covert nature of German U-Boat warfare may have encouraged or even provoked the production of this technology. In addition, high frequency direction finding and radar were especially effective in countering the elusive U-Boats.

        As German U-Boats changed tactics and became more elusive, Allied ships began to have a challenge in honing to find them. Douglas M. McClean expands on the advancement of German U-Boat technology in his article Confronting Technological and Tactical Change: Allied Antisubmarine Warfare in the Last Year of the Battle of the Atlantic. He states, “The new equipment that allowed this dramatic change in U-boat tactics was the schnorkel. This was a comparatively simple device that provided the boats enough air to operate their diesel engines while submerged.” This advancement in technology made it difficult for Allied forces to detect. The invention of the schnorkel made it possible for German U-Boats to evade the Allied forces because the “head of most schnorkels [were] fitted with a detection device that gave warning of the approach of Allied radars.” With being able to detect the approach of Allied forces they had the capability to deep dive into the ocean undetected. Those German U-Boats that were fitted with schnorkels used their detection abilities to engage with non-military vessels, such as shipping vessels. The decision to operate German U-Boats in coastal waters created an effect on maritime activities along the East Coast. With the advancements in submarine technologies, new war counter tactics emerged.

        The sources we have gathered, in concordance with the dataset, will allow us to estimate what sort of losses were incurred as a result of ship hunting by German U-Boats. Additionally, this can be used to investigate other effects that might have occurred due to this practice.

        The significance of this project helps us understand why a select group of foreign military ships were found off the coast of the United States East Coast. This is meaningful information as it examines one of the few times in recent history the United States has been attacked by another military force in the United States borders. The US is certainly familiar with naval combat when it comes to the military, but there are limited situations when civilian vessels are engaged. It is important to examine these data points and consider the significance in how a naval attack would affect the maritime activities today. It is also important to investigate the effectiveness of advancing technology when used by the military. WWII saw the frequent use of sonar in order to detect possible threats.
